I'm a PhD student at the UC Berkeley School of Information where I am advised by Dr. Jenna Burrell and Dr. Niloufar Salehi . My work is primarily centered around what it means to quantify the human. My research broadly explores this within the areas of human-computer interaction, AI accountability, the identity formation in the internet age and the politics of dataset construction. I attend to questions of stratification within technological innovation, methodology as an extension of scientific authority within the contemporary history of data science, and on how communities living within the margins of society use technology as an emancipatory tool. Methodologically, I use a mixture of both quantitative and qualitative intervnetions in addition to ecological thought, critical race theory and science and technology studies to hollistically conduct my research. Furthermore, I am also deeply inspired the citational justice promoted by indigenous ways of knowing. At Berkeley, I'm a member of The Algorithmic Fairness and Opacity Group and the Center for Technology, Policy and Society and the TO3 lab, a social computing lab group led by Dr. Salehi. My research is generously being supported by the Eugene Cota-Robles Fellowship. Prior to joining the I-School, I was a data analyst at an advertising technology company in New York City. I earned my bachelor’s degree in Environmental Policy at Barnard College, Columbia University. When I’m not thinking about research, I moonlight as a recipe developer.

I am currently studying the implications of personanlization and user segmentation within online behavioral targeting. I'm currently investigating how individuals with stigmatized health identities make sense of algorthmic logics. Additionally, I'm investigating new interventions for critical research within social computing.
